Nel midway di this our vita mortal,
I found me in a gloomy wood, astray
Gone from il path direct: ed e’en to tell
It were no easy task, how savage wild
That forest, how robust e rough its growth,
Which to remember only, my dismay
Renews, in bitterness not far from death.
Yet to discourse di what there good befell,
All else will I relate discover’d there.
How first I enter’d it I scarce can say,
Such sleepy dullness in that instant weigh’d
My senses down, when il path true I left,
But when a foot di mountain I reach’d, where clos’d
La valley, that had pierc’d recesses di my heart with dread,
I look’d aloft, e saw his shoulders broad
Already vested with beam di that planet,
Who leads all wanderers safe through every way.

Then was a little respite to la fear,
That in recesses di my heart deep had lain,
All di that night, so pitifully pass’d:
E as a man, with difficult short breath,
Forespent with toiling, ’scap’d from sea to shore,
Turns to il perilous wide waste, e stands
At gaze; e’en so my spirit, that yet fail’d
Struggling with terror, turn’d to view lo straits,
That none hath pass’d e liv’d. My weary frame
After short pause recomforted, again
I journey’d on over that lonely steep,

Il hinder foot still firmer. Scarce l' ascent
Began, when, lo! a panther, nimble, light,
E cover’d with a speckled skin, appear’d,
Nor, when it saw me, vanish’d, rather strove
To check my onward going; that ofttimes
With purpose to retrace my steps I turn’d.

L' hour was prime di morning, e on his way
Aloft il sun ascended with those stars,
That with him rose, when Love divine first mov’d
Those its fair works: so that with joyous hope
All things conspir’d to fill me, la skin gay
Di that swift animal, il matin dawn
E la season sweet. Soon that joy was chas’d,
E by new dread succeeded, when in view
A lion came, ’gainst me, as it appear’d,

With his head held aloft e hunger-mad,
That e’en l' air was fear-struck. A she-wolf
Was at his heels, who in her leanness seem’d
Full di all wants, e many a land hath made
Disconsolate ere now. She with such fear
O’erwhelmed me, at la sight di her appall’d,
That dell' height all hope I lost. As one,
Who with his gain elated, sees il time
When all unwares is gone, he inwardly
Mourns with heart-griping anguish; such was I,
Haunted by that fell beast, never in peace,
Who coming o’er against me, by degrees
Impell’d me where il sun in silence rests.

While to lo lower space with backward step
I fell, my ken discern’d la form di one,
Whose voice seem’d faint through long disuse di speech.
When him in that great desert I espied,
“Have mercy on me!” cried I out aloud,
“Spirit! or living man! what e’er thou be!”

He answer’d: “Now not man, man once I was,
E born di Lombard parents, Mantuana both
By country, when il power di Julius yet
Was scarcely firm. At Rome my vita was past
Beneath il mild Augustus, nel time
Di fabled deities e false. A bard
Was I, e made upright son di Anchises
Il subject di my song, who came from Troy,
When le flames prey’d on haughty towers di Ilium.
But thou, say wherefore to such perils past
Return’st thou? wherefore not this pleasant mount
Ascendest, cause e source di all delight?”

“E art thou then that Virgil, that well-spring,
From which such copious floods di eloquence
Have issued?” I with front abash’d replied.
“Glory e light di all il train tuneful!
May it avail me that I long with zeal
Have sought thy volume, e with love immense
Have conn’d it o’er. My master thou e guide!
Thou he from whom alone I have deriv’d
That style, which for its beauty into fame
Exalts me. See la beast, from whom I fled.
O save me from her, thou illustrious sage!”
